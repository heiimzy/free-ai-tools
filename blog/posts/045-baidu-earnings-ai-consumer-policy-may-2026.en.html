⚡ May 18, 2026 — Evening Flash Briefing
Flash Baidu Earnings AI Policy Sohu Tencent Music

⚡ Baidu AI Revenue Hits Record 42% of Total, China Ministry Plans 'AI + Consumption' Policy, Sohu CEO Skips Foundation Models

This evening's briefing captures a watershed moment for AI monetization in China: Baidu's Q1 report shows AI revenue crossing 42% of total for the first time. Meanwhile, China's Ministry of Commerce is designing an "AI + Consumption" policy framework, and Sohu's CEO offers a contrarian take on the foundation model race.

1. Baidu Q1 2026: AI Revenue Reaches Record 42% of Total, Cloud Business Grows 79% YoY

Baidu reported Q1 2026 revenue of 32.08 billion yuan ($4.4B), with AI-related revenue reaching 13.6 billion yuan — crossing 42% of total for the first time. The figure marks a historic shift: Baidu is no longer primarily a search advertising company. AI has become its core revenue driver.

Baidu AI Cloud was the standout performer, growing 79% year-over-year. In a fiercely competitive Chinese cloud market dominated by Alibaba, Tencent, and Huawei, Baidu has carved out a differentiated position through AI capabilities — model inference, AI-native application deployment, and enterprise AI solutions.

Beyond cloud, Baidu is monetizing AI across its product ecosystem. AI features in search are driving engagement and monetization improvements. Products like Baidu Wangpan (cloud storage) and Baidu Wenku (document sharing) are seeing higher user time-spend and conversion rates after integrating AI capabilities. The thesis is being validated: AI can transform legacy internet businesses, not just create new ones.

For the broader Chinese AI industry, Baidu's results provide a crucial reference point. If a traditional search company can reach 42% AI revenue mix, it suggests AI in China has crossed the chasm from "experimental investment" to "scalable monetization."

2. Ministry of Commerce Drafts "AI + Consumption" Policy Framework

China's Ministry of Commerce announced it is developing an "AI + Consumption" policy framework, designed to accelerate AI technology adoption across consumer-facing sectors. The initiative mirrors the earlier "Internet + Consumption" push but with a fundamental difference: AI's impact is more infrastructural — it's not about moving consumption online, but making consumption intelligent.

Expected policy directions include AI-powered personalized recommendations, intelligent customer service, virtual try-on for fashion and cosmetics, and AI-driven content creation tools integrated into e-commerce platforms. The timing is strategic: with Q1 2026 consumption data showing steady but unspectacular growth, policymakers see AI as a catalyst to unlock new consumer spending.

For e-commerce platforms, brands, and AI service providers, this represents a clear policy tailwind. The framework is still being drafted, but the signal alone is already shaping corporate strategies in China's retail and consumer tech sectors.

3. Sohu CEO Zhang Chaoyang: No Foundation Models, Just AI Applications

In stark contrast to Baidu's full-stack AI strategy, Sohu CEO Zhang Chaoyang stated today that the company has no plans to develop its own foundation models and will instead focus on AI application deployment. The comment stands out in a Chinese tech landscape where nearly every major internet company is racing to build or sponsor a large language model.

Zhang's reasoning is pragmatic: foundation model development requires massive capital, technical talent, and compute infrastructure — not every company needs to build one from scratch. Sohu's approach is to innovate at the application layer, building product experiences on top of existing models.

Whether this strategy pays off remains to be seen, but it raises a legitimate question for the industry: when every company piles into foundation models, some may be better off focusing on applications. After all, in the mobile internet era, the most profitable companies weren't all building operating systems and phones.

Together, these three stories illustrate the diversity of China's AI ecosystem: Baidu represents full-stack vertical integration; the Ministry of Commerce represents policy-driven horizontal enablement; and Sohu represents application-layer focus. All three approaches can coexist and contribute to AI adoption.

4. More Notable Developments

  • Tencent Music completes Ximalaya acquisition: The deal strengthens Tencent Music's position in long-form audio, combining its music streaming business with Ximalaya's podcast and audiobook library. Audio content consolidation is accelerating in China.
  • iQiyi Q1 revenue 6.23B yuan: Membership revenue grew sequentially, but overall growth remains sluggish. Long-form video platforms continue to search for growth drivers amid competition from short video and AI-generated content.
  • Fed hawk Ed Yardeni warns on rate control: The prominent bull argues the Fed should abandon its accommodative stance at the June meeting or risk losing control of interest rates, echoing Warsh's "AI is inflationary" thesis.
  • Commerce Ministry highlights Token economy: A separate analysis piece breaks down emerging tokenomic models, highlighting how AI usage-based pricing is reshaping the economics of digital services.

The Big Picture: AI Monetization's Watershed Moment

Baidu's AI revenue milestone is the most important data point in today's briefing — not just for Baidu, but as evidence that AI can transition from cost center to profit center in a traditional internet company. Combined with the Ministry of Commerce's AI consumption policy signal, China's AI industry is entering a new phase driven by both policy support and commercial viability. And Zhang Chaoyang's contrarian take is a useful reminder: not every company needs to build models. Finding your niche in the AI ecosystem matters more than following the herd.
