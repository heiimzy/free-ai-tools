The Proactive AI Era: Anthropic Orbit, ChatGPT Pulse, and Google's Ambition to Own Your Morning

For the past three years, every major AI product has followed the same interaction pattern: you ask, it answers. The user is always the initiator. The AI is always reactive. That paradigm is now shifting — and May 2026 is the month it broke open.

Within a two-week span, all three frontier AI labs shipped products designed to be proactive — AI that reaches out to you with information before you ask for it. Anthropic launched Orbit (Claude Cowork) on May 6, an autonomous daily briefing agent that pulls from Gmail, Slack, GitHub, Calendar, Drive, and Figma. OpenAI's ChatGPT Pulse rolled out personalized morning briefings to all Pro users. And at Google I/O (May 14-16), Gemini Proactive Assistance was the headline feature — embedding proactive AI across every Google product surface.

Industry analysts have dubbed this the "7 a.m. attention war." The race is on to own the first digital interaction of every user's day — and it has profound implications for productivity, privacy, and the future of AI platforms.

Anthropic Orbit: The Restrained Co-Worker

What It Does

Orbit — officially called "Claude Cowork" — is best understood not as an assistant but as a digital colleague. It doesn't wait for you to ask. Every morning (or at a schedule you set), it delivers a concise briefing drawn from your professional tools:

  • Email (Gmail/Outlook) — surfaces the 5-10 emails that genuinely need your attention, ignoring newsletters, automated notifications, and CC messages
  • Slack/Teams — extracts action items from messages and threads, flags conversations where you've been directly mentioned
  • GitHub/GitLab — summarizes open PRs needing your review, assigned issues, and CI/CD status changes
  • Calendar — generates pre-read materials for today's meetings, flags scheduling conflicts
  • Drive/Notion/Figma — alerts you to documents that need your feedback or approval

The Design Philosophy: Less Is More

What makes Orbit interesting isn't what it does — it's what it doesn't do. Anthropic made several deliberate choices that differentiate Orbit from every proactive AI product that preceded it:

  • No auto-reply. Orbit informs you; it never acts on your behalf. No risk of an AI sending an embarrassing message to your CEO
  • No cross-account blending. Each account's Orbit is siloed. Your work data and personal data don't mix
  • Polling, not streaming. Orbit checks your sources on a schedule rather than continuously monitoring your activity. Lower privacy footprint, lower compute cost
  • Zero-retention after briefing. Raw message content is processed ephemerally. Once the briefing is generated, Anthropic's servers don't keep the source data

This restraint is a strategic choice. Anthropic is betting that enterprise buyers — who control the budgets for AI tools — will prioritize a product that reduces their legal and compliance exposure over one that offers more features but more risk. It's the same "safety-first" brand positioning that has driven Claude's enterprise market share to surpass OpenAI's (34.4% vs 32.3% per Ramp's May 2026 data).

Real-World Feedback

Early adopters consistently highlight one thing: Orbit's ability to connect dots across applications is where it delivers disproportionate value. A Slack message saying "can you review my PR?" automatically resolves to the actual GitHub PR link with status. A meeting invite triggers a search for the relevant documents in Drive. This cross-app reasoning — understanding that "the Figma file Sarah mentioned in Slack" is the same thing as "the design spec in your Project X folder" — is something no notification system has ever done.

The most common complaint? Orbit is too restrained. Some users want it to auto-draft replies or take actions. Anthropic has indicated that an "Orbit Actions" tier is in the pipeline but hasn't shared a timeline.

ChatGPT Pulse: The Information Layer

ChatGPT Pulse (originally launched September 2025, standardized for Pro users in May 2026) takes a different approach. Where Orbit is about work management, Pulse is about information consumption.

What You Get

Each morning, Pulse delivers a personalized briefing that includes:

  • AI-curated news — drawn from your expressed interests, recent chat topics, and browsing history (with permission)
  • Calendar-aware scheduling — if you've connected your calendar, Pulse surfaces today's meetings and relevant prep
  • Deep-read recommendations — long-form articles Pulse thinks you'll find valuable, not clickbait

The key insight behind Pulse is that OpenAI has a massive advantage in understanding your information preferences. Every conversation you've ever had with ChatGPT is a signal about what you care about. Your chat history — the questions you ask, the topics you explore, the articles you paste in for summarization — creates an interest graph that's arguably richer than any social media platform's.

The retention play. Pulse turns ChatGPT from a "tool you use when you have a question" into a "habit you start your day with." OpenAI's internal metrics reportedly show that Pro users who enable Pulse have 40% lower churn than those who don't.

Google Gemini Proactive Assistance: The Ubiquity Play

Google I/O 2026's most significant AI announcement wasn't a new model — it was a new layer across every Google product. Gemini Proactive Assistance embeds itself into:

  • Gmail — auto-detects emails that need replies and surfaces draft suggestions in the sidebar
  • Calendar — generates attendee briefs and relevant document links before each meeting
  • Docs — offers unsolicited writing suggestions when it detects you're stuck (not just when you trigger it with a shortcut)
  • Maps — proactively pushes commute-time traffic alerts based on your Calendar events
  • Photos — surfaces memories and auto-creates albums from recent events without being asked

Google's advantage is surface area. It has billions of users across dozens of products, each of which is a potential touchpoint for proactive AI. No single product may be as deep as Orbit, but the combined reach is unmatched.

The critical difference: Google's proactive layer runs on its own infrastructure. It doesn't need third-party API access to your data — it is the data. Gmail, Calendar, and Drive are Google products, so Gemini can connect them without asking for permissions the way Orbit needs OAuth tokens for each service. This gives Google a lower-friction path to proactive AI, at least within its own ecosystem.

The Productivity Impact: Real Numbers

The promise of proactive AI is that it saves you from the cognitive overhead of context-switching. Every time you check Slack, then Gmail, then GitHub, then Calendar, you pay a "switching cost" — research suggests 23 minutes to fully refocus after an interruption. Proactive AI consolidates all of these into a single briefing, eliminating the need to context-switch during peak morning focus time.

Early data supports this. An internal Anthropic study found that Orbit users saved an average of 12 hours per week on cross-app information triage. OpenAI reports that Pulse users spend 34% less time on news apps and social media in the morning. Google's internal testing suggests Proactive Assistance reduces meeting prep time by 40% for Calendar users.

But there's a counterargument: the filter bubble risk. When an AI decides what's important enough to surface, it's also deciding what to hide. If Orbit decides a particular Slack thread isn't urgent, you never see it — and you never know what you missed. The same dynamic that makes proactive AI powerful also makes it a potential blind spot.

The Privacy Calculus

Proactive AI cannot exist without data access. To know what matters to you, it needs to see what you do. This creates an unavoidable tension: more proactive = more invasive.

Each company is managing this tension differently:

  • Anthropic is betting on enterprise compliance. Orbit's zero-retention architecture is designed to pass corporate security reviews. The trade-off is that Orbit can't learn from past briefings — each day starts fresh
  • OpenAI is betting that users will trade data for convenience. Pulse's personalization improves over time precisely because it retains history. Users can opt out per data source, but the best experience comes from full access
  • Google is betting that existing trust carries over. Many users are already comfortable with Gmail scanning emails for spam filtering and Smart Reply. Proactive Assistance extends this existing relationship rather than asking for new permissions

For enterprise buyers — the people who actually pay for these tools at scale — Anthropic's approach is likely most appealing. For individual users who want maximum convenience, OpenAI's model may win. Google's approach works best for users already deep in its ecosystem.

What's Next: The Morning Briefing Becomes a Platform

The most interesting long-term implication: the morning briefing is becoming a competitive battleground that could reshape the AI industry. Whoever owns the 7 a.m. slot has the opportunity to expand into adjacent use cases — scheduling, task management, shopping, news consumption, and eventually commerce.

Consider the trajectory: today, Orbit tells you about your Slack messages. Tomorrow, it drafts your replies. Next week, it sends them. The line between "informing" and "acting" will blur, and each company's strategy for crossing that line will determine who wins.

Anthropic is moving slowly and deliberately. OpenAI is moving fast and personalizing aggressively. Google is blanketing its ecosystem. By this time next year, "proactive AI" won't be a feature — it will be table stakes. The question isn't whether AI will come to you before you ask. It's which AI will get there first, and whether you'll trust it when it arrives.
