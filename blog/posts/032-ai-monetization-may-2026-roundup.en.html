AI Monetization in May 2026: The Great Revenue Experiment Begins

May 2026 may well be remembered as the month when the AI industry stopped asking "can we build it?" and started asking "how do we get paid for it?" From OpenAI launching its advertising platform to Menlo Ventures projecting $200B in AI revenue, from publishers discovering AI licensing as a lifeline to Wall Street demanding ROI on $800B in CapEx — the conversation has shifted decisively from technology to economics. 1. OpenAI Ads Manager: Building the Next $100B Ad Business

On May 5, OpenAI launched Ads Manager, a self-service advertising platform that lets advertisers place ads directly within ChatGPT. This marks OpenAI's most aggressive monetization move to date — transforming from a subscription company into an advertising powerhouse.

By the numbers:

  • 2026 target: $2.5 billion in ad revenue
  • 2030 target: $100 billion in ad revenue
  • Pilot results: Generated over $100 million in annualized revenue in just six weeks
  • Pricing shift: Removed the $50,000 minimum spend threshold in favor of cost-per-click (CPC) pricing
  • Reach: Ads appear only for free and "Go" plan users — 94% of ChatGPT's 900 million monthly users
  • Partners: Integrated with Dentsu, Omnicom, Publicis, WPP, Adobe, and Criteo

OpenAI CEO Sam Altman has been clear about the strategic logic: free users are the audience, advertisers pay the bills, and premium subscribers get an ad-free experience. It's the classic media playbook — applied to the world's fastest-growing software product. The bet is that ChatGPT's massive engagement (billions of conversations per day) can translate into advertising attention comparable to Google Search or Meta's social feed.

But there are headwinds. ChatGPT's productivity-oriented usage (answering questions, writing code, analyzing documents) makes contextual ad targeting harder than search intent or social interest. And HSBC estimates OpenAI could face a $207 billion cumulative funding gap by 2030 — meaning ads need to work extraordinarily well.

2. Big Tech Q1 2026: Cloud AI Revenue Is Surging — But Is It Enough?

The Big Tech Q1 earnings reports painted a clear picture: AI is driving real revenue, especially in cloud computing. But the scale of capital expenditure is raising uncomfortable questions.

Google Cloud

Q1 revenue $20B (+63% YoY). Contract backlog hit $462B. GenAI product revenue up 800% YoY. The standout performer among the three cloud providers.

Microsoft Azure AI

Annualized revenue run-rate of $37B (+123% YoY). AI services now a material contributor to Azure's overall growth.

Amazon Web Services

Revenue grew 28% YoY. AWS is integrating AI across its entire stack, from Bedrock to CodeWhisperer to QuickSight.

Meta

Revenue beat expectations (+61% net profit), but the market punished the stock after Meta raised CapEx guidance to $145B. The message: great results, eye-watering spending.

The combined AI CapEx from the four hyperscalers now exceeds $800 billion projected through 2026. Wall Street is growing impatient for proof that this spending translates into sustainable, high-margin revenue.

"95% of AI investments have not yet impacted corporate profitability... This is unprecedented and unsustainable." — James Covello, Goldman Sachs

3. Menlo Ventures: AI Revenue Could Reach $200B in 2026

Venture firm Menlo Ventures published a blockbuster projection in May: the world's largest AI companies could generate $200 billion in revenue in 2026, up from $37 billion in 2025 — a 5x increase. In 2024, that figure was just $11.5 billion. The growth curve is a hockey stick rarely seen in technology history.

But the same report contains a sobering statistic: 97% of the 1.8 billion AI users are on free tiers. Only 3% pay for AI tools. Menlo estimates that if even a modest fraction converted to paying $20/month, the addressable market would be $432 billion — versus today's roughly $12 billion in consumer AI subscription revenue.

The bottleneck is clear: AI companies have built extraordinary products that users love, but they haven't yet figured out how to convert massive engagement into proportional revenue. The free-to-paid conversion problem is the defining economic challenge of the AI industry in 2026.

4. Publisher AI Licensing: A New Media Revenue Stream

One of the most unexpected AI monetization stories of May 2026 comes from an unlikely source: traditional media publishers. As AI companies scramble for high-quality training data and real-time content, publishers are discovering that their archives and journalism have become valuable infrastructure assets.

Q1 2026 data points:

  • USA Today Co.: "Other" digital revenue (including AI licensing deals with Meta, Microsoft, and Perplexity) surged 125.6% YoY to $33.75M. CEO Mike Reed said AI deals had a "notable impact" — the first time a major publisher has explicitly credited AI licensing for meaningful revenue growth.
  • News Corp: CEO Robert Thomson now describes the company as an "input company" — positioning quality journalism as essential AI infrastructure akin to semiconductors. Dow Jones is targeting $1 billion in EBITDA within five years, powered by a $250M+ OpenAI deal and a $50M/year Meta agreement.
  • New York Times: Signed a multiyear deal with Amazon worth $20-25M annually, covering news, cooking, and The Athletic content, while simultaneously suing OpenAI and Microsoft for copyright infringement.
  • IAC/People Inc.: Licensing revenue up 26% YoY to $40.7M, driven by the Meta AI deal signed in December 2025.

The AI licensing market is creating a two-tier publishing ecosystem: major players with multi-million dollar deals, and smaller publishers facing 25-50% traffic declines with no AI licensing income to fall back on. The gap is widening fast.

5. Figma: AI Monetization That Actually Works

Figma raised its 2026 revenue forecast to $1.42-1.43B after Q1 revenue hit $333.4M (+139% net revenue retention — the highest in two years). Over 75% of Org/Enterprise users who exceeded their AI credit limits continued using AI credits, suggesting that AI features are driving genuine willingness to pay. Figma's AI ad features — which use generative AI to suggest layouts, generate images, and auto-resize designs — are becoming a meaningful part of the platform's value proposition.

6. The Goldman Sachs Reality Check

Not everyone is celebrating. Goldman Sachs' James Covello published a stark analysis questioning whether AI's massive infrastructure spending will ever translate into proportional revenue:

  • 95% of AI investments have not yet improved corporate profitability
  • Model developers and cloud providers are burning cash while semiconductor companies reap record profits
  • A cited MIT study found most generative AI systems fail to retain feedback, adapt to context, or improve over time

The debate framing the second half of 2026: Is the AI industry in a sustainable growth trajectory, or an infrastructure bubble that will correct when the easy money runs out?

The Bottom Line: AI Monetization in May 2026

Three narratives are competing to define AI monetization:

  1. The Optimist View (Menlo Ventures): We're in the early innings of a revenue supercycle. AI will follow the same path as the internet — massive upfront investment followed by decades of profitable growth.
  2. The Skeptic View (Goldman Sachs): The economics don't add up. $800B in infrastructure spending requires revenue that hasn't materialized and may never materialize at the scale required.
  3. The Pragmatist View: Monetization is real but uneven. Cloud AI, enterprise agents, and content licensing are generating genuine revenue. Consumer AI remains a massive engagement platform with a conversion problem.

The truth probably lies somewhere in the middle. The AI industry has built products that hundreds of millions of people use daily — that's a foundation worth building on. But the path from engagement to revenue is longer and harder than the optimists admit, and the infrastructure costs are higher than the skeptics appreciate.
