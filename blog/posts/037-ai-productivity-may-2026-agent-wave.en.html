AI Productivity in May 2026: The Autonomous Workforce Arrives — ServiceNow, Productive 5.0, and the Agent Engineering Era

May 2026 marked a turning point for enterprise AI productivity. The conversation shifted from "can AI agents work?" to "how do we govern, scale, and secure thousands of them?" This month's major product launches — ServiceNow's autonomous workforce, Productive 5.0's always-on agents, Freshworks' no-code agent studio, and Red Hat's OpenShell — all point to one conclusion: the agent engineering era has arrived. 1. ServiceNow Knowledge 2026: The Autonomous Workforce Debuts

ServiceNow's Knowledge 2026 conference in May delivered what may be the most significant enterprise AI announcement of the year: a full suite of autonomous AI "specialists" spanning IT, HR, finance, legal, procurement, security, and CRM — all governed by a single AI Control Tower.

The numbers are striking. ServiceNow reported that its AI specialists resolve IT service desk cases 99% faster than human agents, with 91% of cases resolved without reassignment. These aren't chatbots answering FAQs — they're autonomous agents that diagnose, escalate, and remediate across multiple systems.

The AI Control Tower, now included in every ServiceNow product by default, provides centralized governance: policy enforcement, audit trails, cost tracking, and performance monitoring across every agent in the enterprise. ServiceNow also expanded its partnership with Microsoft to govern AI agents across both ecosystems — a recognition that agent sprawl is already a real problem.

Why It Matters

ServiceNow's announcement signals that agent governance is no longer optional. When you have hundreds or thousands of autonomous agents making decisions across IT, HR, and finance, you need the same controls you'd have for human employees — maybe tighter. The AI Control Tower model is likely to become the industry standard within 12 months.

2. Productive 5.0: Always-On AI Agents for Professional Services

On May 12, Productive launched version 5.0 of its professional services automation platform, introducing always-on AI agents that autonomously handle the operational backbone of agencies and consultancies: time tracking, meeting transcription, resource allocation, and reporting.

The launch was backed by revealing research: 76% of professionals use AI for content writing, but only 29% use it for operational planning. Productive 5.0 targets exactly this gap — the "last mile" of productivity where strategic work gets bogged down by administrative overhead.

Productive's approach is notable for its narrow focus. Rather than building a general-purpose AI assistant, the agents are purpose-built for professional services workflows. The AI handles the routine so that consultants and agency staff can focus on client strategy and creative work — the high-value tasks that actually drive revenue.

3. Freshworks Freddy AI Agent Studio: No-Code Agent Building

Freshworks entered the agent engineering race with Freddy AI Agent Studio, a no-code platform for building custom AI agents within Freshservice. The studio enables IT teams to create agents that operate across Teams, Slack, and employee portals, connected to backend systems including Workday and Rippling.

Also new: AI Insights (analytics on agent performance and user satisfaction), xLAs (Experience Level Agreements) that measure user experience rather than just uptime, and an MCP Gateway for standardized agent-to-tool communication.

Freshworks' angle is accessibility. By making agent building a no-code, drag-and-drop exercise, they're betting that the teams closest to the problems — not just specialized AI engineers — should be the ones building the agents that solve them.

4. Red Hat AI Factory + NVIDIA: OpenShell for Secure Autonomous Agents

Red Hat and NVIDIA expanded their AI Factory collaboration in mid-May with a focus on a critical but overlooked challenge: how to run long-running autonomous agents securely in production. The answer is OpenShell, a sandboxed runtime environment that gives enterprises policy control over what agents can and cannot do.

Key components of the announcement:

  • OpenShell — sandboxed runtime for autonomous agents with policy-based guardrails
  • Confidential containers — hardware-level security via NVIDIA Confidential Computing, ensuring agent data is encrypted even in memory
  • Model-as-a-Service (MaaS) — on-demand access to NVIDIA Nemotron models within the Red Hat OpenShift environment
  • AI blueprints — pre-built, production-tested agent architectures for common enterprise use cases

The Red Hat/NVIDIA partnership addresses a specific pain point: most enterprises are comfortable deploying AI for inference but not for autonomous decision-making. OpenShell's sandbox model provides the security boundary that enterprise risk teams demand.

5. Okta + AWS: The Identity Layer for AI Agents

Okta's May 2026 integration with Amazon Bedrock AgentCore tackled another foundational challenge: identity and access management for non-human workers. If an AI agent is making decisions across your SaaS stack, how do you control what it can access, and how do you audit what it did?

Okta for AI Agents delivers:

  • Shadow AI agent discovery — find agents that were deployed without IT knowledge
  • Least-privilege enforcement — agents get the minimum permissions needed, not blanket access
  • Privileged credential management — rotating credentials for agent-to-system authentication
  • Full audit logging — every action an agent takes is recorded for compliance

This is a sign that the identity industry is waking up to the "non-human identity" problem. By 2027, there could be more AI agents than human employees in many large enterprises — and each one needs an identity.

6. Databricks AI Day: The 30-40% Productivity Signal

At Databricks AI Day 2026 in early May, executives from Databricks and Microsoft presented concrete productivity data from enterprise agentic AI deployments. The headline: 30-40% productivity gains are being reported by early adopters.

IDC data shared at the event revealed that 88.1% of financial services firms are already using or planning agentic AI deployments. The warning from Databricks: governance is "the single most important enabler" for agentic AI — without it, organizations risk agent sprawl, security incidents, and compliance failures.

Boomi and Couchbase also announced a partnership in May focused on production-ready enterprise AI agents, combining Boomi's connectivity and governance layer with Couchbase's operational data platform for real-time, memory-speed context.

7. The Five Trends Defining AI Productivity in May 2026

Taking stock of the month's announcements, five clear themes emerge:

  1. Agent governance is the #1 priority — AI Control Towers (ServiceNow, Boomi, Okta) are becoming essential infrastructure. Every major platform now ships governance as a default feature, not an add-on.
  2. No-code agent building democratizes creation — Freshworks and others are proving that the best people to build agents are the domain experts closest to the problem, not AI specialists.
  3. Security is shifting left — Red Hat's OpenShell, Okta's identity layer, and NVIDIA's confidential computing all point to security being baked into the agent runtime, not bolted on after deployment.
  4. Measurable ROI is emerging — ServiceNow's 99% faster resolution, Databricks' 30-40% productivity gains, and Productive's administrative automation are replacing vague "efficiency" claims with concrete metrics.
  5. From single agents to agent systems — The conversation is moving from "how well does one agent perform?" to "how do hundreds of agents coordinate, share context, and stay governed?" This is the agent engineering paradigm shift.

The Bottom Line

May 2026 was the month enterprise AI productivity moved from experimentation to industrialization. The product launches from ServiceNow, Productive, Freshworks, and Red Hat — combined with the governance infrastructure from Okta and Boomi — provide the complete stack for building, deploying, and managing AI agents at scale.

The organizations that will win are not necessarily those with the most advanced AI models. They're the ones that invest in governance, security, and the operational discipline to run agents safely in production. The technology is ready. The question now is whether enterprise processes are.
