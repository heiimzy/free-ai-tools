1. Figma Q1 2026: The Case Study in AI Monetization Done Right

Figma reported Q1 2026 earnings on May 14, and the numbers are striking: $333 million in revenue, up 46% year-over-year — its second consecutive quarter of accelerating growth. Net dollar retention hit 139%, the highest in over two years.

The driver? AI feature monetization. Figma's AI capabilities — Figma Make (design automation) and Figma Weave (prototyping) — are driving both seat upgrades and AI credit consumption. Approximately 60% of customers with over $100K in ARR are using Make Weekly. The CFO noted that AI credit monetization is running "ahead of expectations."

Figma's approach is instructive: rather than giving AI away free to drive adoption (the approach 58% of SaaS companies take), Figma layered AI usage charges on top of seat-based subscriptions. Users pay for the base product, then buy AI credits for premium features. This hybrid model — seat + consumption — is emerging as the most effective structure for AI monetization in 2026.

2. The Pricing Revolution: Separate AI Charging Wins

A landmark study by SBIGrowth in May 2026 analyzed the pricing strategies of hundreds of SaaS companies and produced a stark finding: 58% of software companies bundle AI into core packages at no charge — and those companies miss revenue targets at 2x the rate of companies that charge separately for AI.

The data is unambiguous:

  • Companies that separately monetize AI meet or exceed targets 84% of the time
  • Companies that bundle AI for free miss targets at double the rate
  • Seat-based pricing remains the strongest forecasting anchor — abandoning it without pricing maturity leads to 47% target misses

The winning formula, according to the research: layer separate AI usage charges on a seat-dominant base. This approach achieved a 47% exceedance rate — the highest of any model tested.

The implication is clear: giving AI away for free is not just leaving money on the table — it's actively harming revenue predictability. As compute costs remain high, companies that fail to pass through at least some of those costs are building an unsustainable business.

3. The 6 Emerging AI Pricing Models of 2026

May 2026 saw the crystallization of distinct AI pricing models across the industry. Here's the current landscape:

1. Consumption/Usage-Based

Token-based, message-based, or API-call pricing. Used by OpenAI API, Deepgram, and most model providers. Best for variable usage patterns but creates revenue unpredictability.

2. Base + Usage (Hybrid)

Fixed subscription + metered overage above a threshold. Used by ChatGPT Plus/Pro/Business and Figma. The emerging gold standard — predictable base revenue with upside from power users.

3. Outcome/KPI-Based

AI priced on measurable business outcomes — "reduce defect rate by 30%" or "increase conversion by 15%." Emerging in manufacturing and enterprise AI, but requires sophisticated measurement infrastructure.

4. Agent-Based Billing

Charging per completed goal or task rather than per prompt or per token. Early stage but gaining traction with autonomous coding agents and AI workflow platforms.

5. Prepaid Credits / Wallets

Customers buy credits upfront and draw down with usage. Popular in design tools (Figma AI Credits) and no-code AI platforms. Good for customer commitment but creates accounting complexity.

6. Advertising-Supported

Free AI access monetized through ads. OpenAI launched Ads Manager in May 2026 targeting $2.4B in ad revenue. Netflix-style ad tiers entering AI — ChatGPT's $8/mo "Go" plan in India captured 16.5% of global visitors.

4. Anthropic vs. Microsoft: Two Competing AI Business Models

An analysis from FourWeekMBA in May 2026 highlighted the fundamental strategic divergence in AI monetization:

  • Anthropic's "AI-as-a-Service" model: Charge per conversation, differentiate on safety and capability. Carries full legal and copyright liability but captures premium pricing. Projected revenue growth of 10-80x in 2026 driven by enterprise API and Claude Code.
  • Microsoft's "AI-as-Feature" model: Embed AI into existing products (M365 Copilot, GitHub Copilot), increase subscription prices, distribute risk across a $200B+ revenue base. Lower per-user AI revenue but vastly larger installed base.

The analysis concludes that integration moats may outlast safety moats for long-term monetization durability — Microsoft's distribution advantage is harder to replicate than Anthropic's capability advantage.

5. The ROI Test: Where AI Monetization Works (and Where It Doesn't)

Creative Strategies published a critical framework in May 2026 entitled "From AI Usage to AI Earnings Power." Their core argument: the durability of the AI monetization cycle depends entirely on measurable customer ROI, not raw capability.

Strongest ROI evidence found in:

  • Contact centers — measurable handle-time reduction
  • IT access management — reduced ticket volume
  • Developer workflows — faster cycle times
  • Compliance and legal — lower review costs
  • Enterprise search — reduced lookup time

Weakest ROI evidence:

  • Sales/RevOps — attribution remains fuzzy
  • HR — difficult to isolate AI impact
  • Broad knowledge-worker assistants — productivity gains are real but hard to quantify in dollar terms

Their key insight: "The monetization claim becomes easier to evaluate when AI changes cost, speed, quality, or capacity of measurable work units." If you can't point to which of these four dimensions your AI improves — and by how much — you don't have a monetization strategy, you have a feature.
