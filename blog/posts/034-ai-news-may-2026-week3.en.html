May Week 3 AI News: GPT-5.5 Goes Free, Mythos Finds Thousands of Zero-Days, Apple's $250M AI Lesson

The third week of May 2026 delivered a remarkably diverse set of AI stories — from OpenAI making its latest model completely free, to Anthropic's gated supermodel uncovering thousands of security vulnerabilities, to Apple paying a quarter-billion dollars for overpromising AI features. Here's what happened and why it matters.

1. OpenAI GPT-5.5 Instant: Free for Everyone, and Actually Better

On May 6, OpenAI made GPT-5.5 Instant the default ChatGPT model — and made it available to everyone, including free-tier users. This is the first time OpenAI has given free users immediate access to its latest flagship model, marking a strategic shift in an increasingly competitive landscape where Google Gemini and Anthropic Claude are applying relentless pressure.

What improved:

  • AIME 2025 Math: 81.2 (up from 65.4) — a 24% jump
  • MMMU-Pro Multimodal Reasoning: 76.0 (up from 69.2)
  • GPQA PhD-Level Science: 85.6% (up from 78.5%)
  • Hallucination reduction: 52.5% fewer fabricated claims in medicine, law, and finance
  • Conciseness: ~30% fewer words, significantly fewer gratuitous emojis

The headline feature is the dramatic reduction in hallucination. OpenAI claims GPT-5.5 Instant produces 52.5% fewer hallucinated statements in high-stakes domains. This isn't just a marginal improvement — it changes the calculus for professional use cases where trustworthiness has been the primary barrier to adoption.

New "Memory Sources" transparency features show users exactly why the model made a particular suggestion. Personalization capabilities (rolling out first to Plus/Pro users) allow the model to draw context from past conversations, uploaded files, and connected Gmail accounts.

OpenAI also released GPT-5.5-Cyber, a specialized version for vetted cybersecurity professionals, and GPT-Rosalind, a biology-tuned model. Internally, the company reportedly discussed spinning out its robotics and consumer hardware divisions — a plan that was ultimately rejected.

2. Anthropic Mythos: The Model That Was Too Capable to Release

Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview — a gated model released only to ~50 organizations under Project Glasswing — continued to generate extraordinary results this month. Its internal benchmark scores are remarkable: 93.9% on SWE-bench Verified, 94.6% on GPQA Diamond, and 83.1% on CyberGym (a full 10 points ahead of the publicly available Opus 4.7).

What makes Mythos different is not just its capability — it's that Anthropic intentionally chose not to release it broadly due to its offensive cyber capabilities. The public Opus 4.7 model was "differentially reduced" during training to remove dangerous cyber abilities.

This week's Mythos developments:

Palo Alto Networks Discloses 26 CVEs

On May 14, Palo Alto Networks disclosed 26 CVEs (75 total issues) across 130+ products discovered using Mythos and GPT-5.5-Cyber — 5x their normal monthly volume. Their warning to the industry: defenders have only 3-5 months before attackers gain similar AI capabilities.

UK AISI: Mythos "Evolving Faster Than Expected"

The UK AI Security Institute reported that a newer Mythos checkpoint became the first model ever to solve the "Cooling Tower" cyber range. Their broader finding: AI cyber task completion capability is doubling every 4.7 months.

macOS M5 Breached

Security researchers used Mythos-assisted exploit development to achieve the first public macOS kernel memory corruption exploit on Apple's M5 silicon.

Project Glasswing now spans 12 launch partners (AWS, Apple, Google, Microsoft, NVIDIA, JPMorganChase, and others), 40+ additional organizations, and has provided $100M in usage credits plus $4M in direct donations to open-source security foundations. Mozilla independently reported finding and patching 271 vulnerabilities in Firefox with Mythos assistance.

3. Apple's $250M Settlement: The Cost of AI Hype

Apple agreed to pay $250 million to settle a class-action lawsuit over misleading advertisements for iPhone 16's AI features. The core issue: Apple's ads showcased Apple Intelligence and Siri capabilities that were significantly delayed or never delivered at launch.

The settlement sends a clear message to the entire tech industry: as AI features become central to product marketing, the gap between what companies promise and what they deliver creates real legal liability. With regulators worldwide tightening scrutiny on AI product claims, Apple's $250M lesson is one that every company launching AI features should heed.

4. DeepSeek V4: Closing the Gap, Raising $45B

DeepSeek released a preview of DeepSeek V4, reportedly approaching frontier-model performance at a fraction of the cost. More striking is the reported fundraising: roughly $45 billion in the company's first external funding round — a figure that would make it one of the largest private capital raises in tech history. If successful, this would validate the thesis that open-weight models can compete with the best proprietary systems while fundamentally reshaping the economics of AI inference.

5. Cerebras IPO: 70% First-Day Surge

AI chip company Cerebras went public with a bang, with shares surging approximately 70% on day one. The strong debut signals continued investor appetite for AI hardware companies that can challenge NVIDIA's dominance. Cerebras's wafer-scale architecture has found particular traction in government and scientific computing workloads where its single-wafer design offers unique advantages over traditional GPU clusters.

6. NIST CAISI: Government Pre-Deployment Testing Goes Live

The US National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST), through its CAISI program, signed formal agreements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and xAI for pre-deployment government evaluation of frontier AI models. This represents the first operationalization of the government's AI testing framework — a significant step toward the kind of pre-release safety reviews that the White House is reportedly considering codifying through executive order. The model is likened to FDA drug approval: models would need to pass government safety evaluations before broad public release.

7. The 18-Month Prediction: White-Collar Automation

Microsoft AI chief Mustafa Suleyman made a provocative prediction: all computer-based white-collar work could be automated within 18 months. While Suleyman's timelines have been aggressive before, the statement reflects a genuine acceleration in AI's capability to handle complex knowledge-work tasks. Whether 18 months or 18 years, the direction of travel is clear — and enterprises that aren't planning for this transition are already behind.

Key Takeaways for the Week

  1. The trust threshold is breaking — GPT-5.5's 52.5% hallucination reduction may be the point where AI becomes reliable enough for high-stakes professional use
  2. AI-powered cyber offense is running ahead of defense — Palo Alto's 3-5 month warning window is shrinking, and Mythos's capability doubling every 4.7 months accelerates the timeline
  3. Government AI testing is becoming operational — NIST CAISI agreements mark the start of a new regulatory era for frontier models
  4. Marketing overpromise has consequences — Apple's $250M settlement sets a precedent for AI feature advertising
  5. Hardware competition is real — Cerebras's 70% IPO surge proves investors see room beyond NVIDIA
